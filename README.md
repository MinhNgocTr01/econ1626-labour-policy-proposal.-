## Overview

A 1,200-word evidence-based policy proposal advising the Australian
federal government on managing AI's structural impact on the labour
market. The proposal argues that the core policy failure is
time-asymmetric: displacement is front-loaded and concentrated on
middle-skill cohorts, while reinstatement is back-loaded and diffuse.
Current policy settings address neither dimension.

The recommended package: wage insurance, demand-verified reskilling,
AI complementarity investment in public services, and a productivity
levy — is phased over seven years at an estimated $1.74–2.08 billion,
approximately 0.2% of the $0.8 trillion GDP loss projected under no
intervention.

---

## Scope & Limitations

This proposal focuses on three Australian worker cohorts identified as
highest-exposure: white-collar and professional services, retail and
hospitality, and gig economy workers. It does not model macroeconomic
feedback effects, international labour mobility, or sector-specific
enterprise bargaining implications. Fiscal estimates are scenario-based
and would require Productivity Commission modelling before informing
budget decisions. The proxy eligibility framework proposed here would
require further legal design and consultation before legislative
implementation.

---

## Analytical Framework

The impact analysis is structured around the **Autor-Levy-Murnane (ALM)
task taxonomy (2003)**, extended to account for generative AI's capacity
to perform non-routine analytic tasks — a departure from the conditions
under which the original framework was developed. Policy options are
evaluated systematically against five criteria: causal alignment, target
precision, reinstatement effect, equity, and fiscal efficiency. This
criteria-based evaluation is intended to make trade-off reasoning
explicit and auditable, not to produce a single optimal answer.
